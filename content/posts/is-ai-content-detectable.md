"Is AI content detectable?" is the question behind a lot of anxiety for writers and students. The honest answer: partly, but not reliably. Here's how detection actually works.

## How AI detectors work

[AI detectors](/posts/best-ai-detectors) don't "know" who wrote something. They estimate a **probability** based on statistical patterns:

- **Perplexity** — how predictable the next word is (AI text is often very predictable)
- **Burstiness** — how much sentence length and structure vary (humans vary more)
- **Phrasing patterns** — generic, even, cliché-heavy text reads as AI

A score is a guess from these signals — not a record of how the text was made.

## Why detection is unreliable

| Problem | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| False positives | Human writing flagged as AI |
| False negatives | Edited AI text passes as human |
| Bias | Non-native and formulaic writing over-flagged |
| Arms race | Models and detectors constantly shift |

Because of this, every reputable source warns against using a detector score as **sole proof**.

## What makes content harder to detect

Not tricks — substance. Text with **varied rhythm, real specifics, first-hand detail and a clear voice** looks human because it *is* the kind of thing humans write. That's also what makes content genuinely good, which is why [humanizing AI text](/posts/how-to-humanize-ai-text) and "writing well" are mostly the same skill.

## What this means for you

- **Students:** know your school's policy; a flag can happen even on honest work, so keep drafts and notes.
- **Writers/SEO:** Google targets *unhelpful* content, not AI assistance per se — focus on adding real value, not on dodging detectors.
- **Anyone accusing on a score:** don't. Corroborate first.

## Bottom line

AI content is detectable in part but never with certainty — detectors output probabilities and get it wrong both ways. The durable takeaway isn't how to evade them; it's that genuinely original, specific, well-voiced writing both reads better and looks human, because it is.
