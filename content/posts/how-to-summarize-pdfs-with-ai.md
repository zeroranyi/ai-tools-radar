Long PDFs — reports, research papers, contracts — eat hours. AI can turn them into a clear summary in minutes. Here's how to do it accurately.

## Step 1: Pick a tool that handles files

Use an assistant that accepts **PDF uploads** — ChatGPT or Claude both do. For **long documents**, Claude's large context window is especially useful.

## Step 2: Ask for the right kind of summary

Don't just say "summarize this." Be specific:

- "Give me the **5 key takeaways**"
- "Summarize **section by section**"
- "What are the **main conclusions and any caveats**?"
- "Explain this to a **non-expert**"

## Step 3: Demand accuracy

- Ask it to **quote or cite** the sections behind key claims
- For anything important, **verify against the original**
- Watch for **missed nuance** in long or technical docs

## Step 4: Go deeper with follow-ups

Once summarized, ask follow-ups — "expand on point 3," "what does it say about X?" — to drill in without rereading the whole thing.

## Quick tool guide

| Need | Best pick |
| --- | --- |
| Long documents / papers | Claude |
| General PDF Q&A | ChatGPT |
| Many documents at once | A RAG-based tool |

> **The accuracy habit that saves you:** treat an AI summary as a *fast first pass*, never the final word — especially for contracts, research or anything you'll act on. AI can quietly drop a key caveat or misstate a number. Asking it to quote the exact passage behind each important point turns a vague summary into something you can verify in seconds. Speed plus a spot-check beats blind trust.

## Bottom line

Summarize PDFs by uploading to ChatGPT or Claude (Claude for long ones), asking for a structured summary, and verifying key claims against the source. Fast first pass, human check on what matters.
