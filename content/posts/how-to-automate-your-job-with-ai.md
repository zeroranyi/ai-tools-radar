You probably spend hours a week on tasks AI could handle. Automating them isn't about replacing yourself — it's about freeing time for the work that actually matters. Here's how.

## Step 1: List your repetitive tasks

For one week, note what you do repeatedly: emails, reports, data entry, scheduling, summaries, status updates. **The repetitive, rules-based tasks are your automation targets.**

## Step 2: Automate the biggest time-sinks

| Task type | Tool | What it does |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Writing & email | ChatGPT / Claude | Draft, reply, summarize |
| Data & admin | AI in your apps | Clean, categorize, report |
| Multi-step workflows | Zapier / Make | Connect apps, trigger actions |
| Meetings | AI note-takers | Transcribe & summarize |

## Step 3: Start with writing (the fastest win)

Drafting emails, documents and summaries with a general assistant requires zero setup and saves hours immediately. Begin here.

## Step 4: Build workflow automations

Once comfortable, use **Zapier or Make** to automate multi-step processes — e.g. "new form submission → summarize → log it → notify me." This is where bigger time savings come from.

Automate the *busywork*, invest the saved time in the *valuable work*. AI rarely replaces a whole job, but it does replace people who refuse to use it with people who do. The winners use AI to clear the repetitive 50% of their week and pour that time into judgment, strategy, relationships and creative work — the things AI can't do and that make you harder to replace. Automation is a promotion strategy, not a redundancy risk, if you use the freed time well.

## Bottom line

Automate your job with AI by targeting repetitive tasks: writing with ChatGPT/Claude, workflows with Zapier/Make, meetings with note-takers. Free the busywork, double down on high-value work — that's how AI makes you more valuable, not less.
