AI can cut blog writing time dramatically — or produce generic filler that ranks nowhere. The difference is the workflow. Here's how to use AI for speed while keeping what makes a post worth reading.

## The workflow that works

1. **Research with AI** — gather angles, questions to answer, and subtopics.
2. **Outline** — have AI propose a structure; you reshape it around your angle.
3. **First draft** — let AI draft sections fast.
4. **Heavily edit** — this is where the value is added (see below).
5. **Fact-check** — verify every claim, stat and name.
6. **Polish** — title, intro, formatting, internal links.

## The editing step is the whole game

Raw AI drafts are generic because the AI doesn't have *your* experience. Make it yours:

- Add **first-hand experience** and opinions
- Insert **specific real examples** and data
- Cut **generic filler** ("In today's fast-paced world…")
- **Vary sentence length**; read it aloud
- Give the post a **clear point of view**

## Why this matters for ranking

| Raw AI post | AI-assisted, human-edited |
| --- | --- |
| Generic, templated | Specific, original |
| No real experience | First-hand insight |
| Risk of being unhelpful | Genuinely useful |
| Ranks poorly | Has a real shot |

Treating the AI draft as the finish line instead of the starting line. Google's helpful-content systems and readers both punish generic AI filler. The post that ranks is the one where AI did the grunt work and *you* added the experience, examples and voice only you have. AI gets you a fast 70%; the human 30% is the part that earns the ranking and the trust.

## Bottom line

Write a blog post with AI by using it for research, outline and draft — then editing hard to add originality, accuracy and voice. AI for speed, human for substance: that's how AI-assisted posts read well and rank.
