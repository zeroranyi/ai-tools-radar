"Is ChatGPT safe?" usually means two different things: is it safe for your *data*, and is it safe for your *device*. The device risk is low; the data risk is the one to manage. Here's the practical picture.

## Is your data safe?

This is the real question. By default:

- Conversations are **generally stored**
- On consumer plans, they **may be used to improve models** unless you opt out
- **Enterprise/Team** plans offer stronger data controls and no training on your data

So the rule is simple: **don't paste anything you wouldn't want retained** — passwords, financial details, client data, confidential company information.

## What to do (practical safety steps)

| Risk | What to do |
| --- | --- |
| Data retention/training | Turn off chat history/training in settings |
| Sensitive work data | Use Team/Enterprise, or an approved tool |
| Account compromise | Strong unique password + 2FA |
| Fake "ChatGPT" sites/apps | Use only the official site/app |
| Oversharing | Treat every message as potentially stored |

## Is your device safe?

Using ChatGPT itself won't give you malware. The device-level risks are **phishing sites and fake apps** impersonating ChatGPT, and **account takeover** — both solved by sticking to official apps, a strong password and two-factor authentication.

## Is the content safe to trust?

Separate from privacy: ChatGPT can be confidently wrong ([hallucinate](/posts/what-is-ai-hallucination)). "Safe" also means not blindly trusting outputs — verify important facts.

## Bottom line

ChatGPT is safe to use if you manage the data side: opt out of training, keep sensitive and confidential information out of consumer accounts, use Enterprise/Team for work data, and secure your account. The biggest real-world risk is oversharing, not malware. If privacy is paramount, consider [running a model locally](/posts/how-to-run-a-local-llm).
