"Open-source AI" is everywhere in 2026, but the term is used loosely. Here's what it really means and when it's the right choice.

## What is open-source AI?

**Open-source AI** generally means a model whose **weights are publicly available** — you can download, run, modify and often commercially use it. Examples include **Llama, DeepSeek, Mistral and Qwen**.

This contrasts with **closed models** (like GPT) that you can only access through an API.

> Note: "open weights" doesn't always mean the training data and full code are open too. Openness is a spectrum — check each model's actual license.

## Open vs closed

| | Open-source AI | Closed AI |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Access | Download & run | API only |
| Privacy | You control data | Sent to provider |
| Cost | Hardware, no per-token | Per-token |
| Customization | Full (fine-tune) | Limited |
| Peak capability | Strong, improving | Often leads |
| Convenience | You manage it | Easiest |

## Pros of open-source AI

- **Privacy** — data stays on your infrastructure
- **No per-token fees** — cheaper at scale
- **Customization** — fine-tune freely
- **No vendor lock-in** — you own the stack

## Cons

- **You manage the hardware and ops**
- **Top capability** may trail the best closed models
- **Support** is community-based, not a vendor SLA

Open-source AI isn't "better" or "worse" — it's a different trade. You gain control, privacy and cost savings; you give up convenience and a bit of peak capability. Businesses with privacy requirements or high volume increasingly choose open models; people who just want the smartest model with zero setup stick with closed APIs. Match it to your constraints, not the ideology.

## Bottom line

Open-source AI means downloadable, modifiable models (Llama, DeepSeek, Mistral) that trade convenience for control, privacy and cost savings. Choose it when those matter; choose closed APIs when you want peak capability with no infrastructure.
