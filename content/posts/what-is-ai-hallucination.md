If you've ever caught an AI stating a fake statistic or inventing a citation with total confidence, you've seen a hallucination. Here's why it happens and how to stop trusting the wrong answers.

## What is an AI hallucination?

An **AI hallucination** is when a model generates information that is **false but sounds confident and plausible**. Invented facts, fake quotes, non-existent sources — delivered with the same tone as correct answers.

## Why it happens

Language models predict **likely text**, not **verified facts**. When the model doesn't actually have the right information, it doesn't say "I don't know" — it generates the most plausible-sounding continuation, which can be wrong. It has no built-in sense of its own certainty.

## Common examples

- Invented statistics or dates
- Fake citations and URLs that don't exist
- Confident answers about events it has no real data on
- Made-up product features or pricing

## How to reduce hallucinations

1. **Ask for sources** — and actually check them.
2. **Ground it** — paste the source material or use RAG so it works from real text.
3. **Use web-connected tools** for current facts.
4. **Invite uncertainty** — tell it to say when it's not sure.
5. **Verify high-stakes claims** yourself, always.

> **The reframe that protects you:** don't think of hallucination as a rare bug — think of it as the *default risk* of a system that predicts text. Once you treat every factual claim as "probably right, worth checking," you get the speed of AI without getting burned. The people who get embarrassed by AI are the ones who assumed it was a fact engine.

## Bottom line

A hallucination is confident-sounding fiction from an AI. It's an inherent trait of how LLMs work, not a glitch — so use grounding and verification, and never outsource your fact-checking entirely to the model.
