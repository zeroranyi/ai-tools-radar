GPTZero is the AI detector most people meet first, especially in schools. This review covers what it does well, where it fails, and how to read its results honestly.

## What GPTZero does

It analyzes text and estimates the **probability** it was AI-generated, using signals like predictability and sentence variation. It's accessible, has a free tier, and is widely adopted in education.

## How accurate is it?

Good at catching **raw, unedited AI text**; unreliable everywhere else. Like every detector, it produces:

| Error type | What happens |
| --- | --- |
| False positive | Human writing flagged as AI |
| False negative | Edited AI text passes as human |
| Bias | Non-native/formulaic writing over-flagged |

No detector — GPTZero included — is reliable enough to treat as proof. For the why, see [is AI content detectable](/posts/is-ai-content-detectable).

## Pricing

Free tier with limited checks; paid plans add volume, API and team features. Confirm current numbers on GPTZero's site.

## How to use it responsibly

- Treat the score as a **signal**, not a verdict
- **Never accuse** on a percentage alone — corroborate
- Remember [humanized or edited AI](/posts/how-to-humanize-ai-text) often passes
- For comparison shopping, see [best AI detectors](/posts/best-ai-detectors)

## Bottom line

GPTZero is a capable, accessible detector that's useful as a first signal — but it's not proof, it produces false results in both directions, and it should never be the sole basis for a decision about someone's work. Use it with judgment. Confirm current pricing on GPTZero's official site.
