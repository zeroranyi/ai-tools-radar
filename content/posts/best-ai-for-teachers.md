AI can give teachers back hours of prep time each week — if used thoughtfully. Here are the tools worth using, grouped by task, with honest guidance on where to keep the human in charge.

## The teacher stack by task

| Task | Best AI | Free? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lesson planning | ChatGPT / Claude | Yes |
| Worksheets & materials | ChatGPT / Claude | Yes |
| Quizzes & differentiation | Education-specific tools | Varies |
| Feedback drafts | Claude / ChatGPT | Yes |

## Lesson planning and materials

A general assistant can draft a **lesson plan, worksheet, rubric or activity** in minutes — then you adapt it to your class. This is the biggest time-saver and a great starting point.

## Differentiation and quizzes

Ask AI to **adapt a text to different reading levels**, generate **practice questions**, or create **variations** for different learners. Specialized education tools streamline this further.

## Feedback

AI can draft **feedback starters** and help keep comments consistent across a class — then you add your judgment and personal knowledge of the student.

> **The responsible-use line for educators:** AI is a brilliant *prep assistant* and a poor *final authority*. Always check materials for accuracy (it can be confidently wrong), keep grading judgment with you (especially for subjective or high-stakes work), and follow your school's AI policy. Used this way, AI frees you to spend less time on paperwork and more on actual teaching — which is the whole point.

## How to start

1. Use **ChatGPT or Claude** (free tier) for plans, materials and feedback drafts.
2. Add an **education-specific tool** if you want quiz generation and differentiation at scale.
3. Always **review and adapt** — you're the expert on your students.
